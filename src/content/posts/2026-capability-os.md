Software engineers spend a lot of time designing systems.

We think about architecture, interfaces, dependencies, resilience, feedback loops and long-term evolution. We invest huge amounts of effort building platforms that help other engineers become more effective.

But I’ve realised something curious.

Most of us apply that thinking everywhere except to ourselves.

Our careers often evolve through a series of disconnected decisions. Learn Kubernetes. Take an AWS certification. Start writing. Build a homelab. Experiment with AI.

Individually, they’re all worthwhile. Collectively, though, they can become an unstructured collection of activities rather than a coherent system.

Over the last few years I’ve started thinking about this differently.

Instead of asking, “What should I learn next?”, I increasingly ask a higher-level question.

What capabilities do I want to build, and how should they reinforce one another over the next decade?

That question gradually led me to what I now think of as a Capability Operating System.

## Skills are temporary. Capabilities endure.

A skill is usually tied to a particular technology or context.

Terraform. GitHub Actions. Kubernetes. CloudFormation.

Capabilities are different.

Systems thinking. Technical judgement. Clear writing. Architectural design. Learning quickly. Making good decisions under uncertainty.

Technologies change. Capabilities transfer.

Every time our industry shifts, engineers with strong underlying capabilities adapt far more quickly than those whose expertise is tightly coupled to a particular implementation.

That’s why I’ve become much more interested in optimising for capabilities than individual skills.

## Think in capability graphs, not skill trees

Learning is often described as a tree.

Master one thing, then move to the next.

My experience has been much messier—and much more interesting.

It’s closer to a graph.

Writing improves thinking. Thinking improves architecture. Architecture improves platform engineering. Reflection improves judgement. Teaching strengthens understanding.

Each capability reinforces several others.

The interesting question isn’t simply:

What should I learn?

It’s:

Which capability will create the greatest positive spillover into everything else?

Those are unusually good investments because the return extends far beyond the original project.

## Capability ROI

That way of thinking has quietly changed how I choose projects.

Traditionally, I might have asked questions like:

* Will I use this?
* Will it save me time?
* Does this seem interesting?

Those questions still matter, but they no longer come first.

Instead, I try to ask:

* What capability will this permanently strengthen?
* What future opportunities could it unlock?
* Which other capabilities will improve as a result?
* Who will I become by building this?

I’ve started thinking of that as Capability ROI.

The output still matters.

The capability gained often matters more.

Your projects should build you

Looking back, many of my favourite projects weren’t really about the technology they appeared to be about.

Building a personal cloud wasn’t primarily about replacing SaaS or self-hosting for its own sake. It gave me an environment where I could make architectural decisions from first principles, operate production-like services, practise identity management, networking, automation, disaster recovery and observability, and experiment without organisational constraints.

Starting a technical blog wasn’t primarily about publishing articles. It forced me to organise my thinking, explain ideas clearly, identify gaps in my own understanding and develop a habit of turning experience into reusable knowledge.

Even rebuilding my GitHub profile became less about showcasing repositories and more about demonstrating how I think: reusable tooling, infrastructure patterns, documentation and experiments rather than isolated code samples.

Viewed individually, those projects look unrelated.

Viewed together, they’re all strengthening the same operating system.

Each one develops capabilities that make the next project easier, more ambitious and ultimately more valuable.

## Every capability has a lifecycle

Although the projects differ, I’ve found that capabilities develop through remarkably similar cycles.

Orient
↓
Build mental models
↓
Deliberate practice
↓
Build something
↓
Receive feedback
↓
Reflect
↓
Capture what matters
↓
Teach
↓
Capability strengthened

The technology changes.

The lifecycle doesn’t.

That was a surprisingly important realisation.

Instead of designing a new learning process every time I encountered a new technology or discipline, I could focus on improving the learning system itself.

Reflection is where experience becomes judgement

Experience alone doesn’t produce capability.

We’ve all met people with years of experience who seem to have repeated the same year twenty times.

The difference, I think, is reflection.

After finishing a project, I try to ask questions like:

* What surprised me?
* What assumptions changed?
* What mistakes would I avoid next time?
* What principle emerged?
* What should become reusable?

Those questions don’t simply help me understand the project I just completed.

They improve how I approach the next one.

That’s where experience begins to turn into judgement.

## Every experience should leave an artifact

One habit that’s become increasingly important is refusing to let useful experiences disappear.

A new service in my personal cloud isn’t finished simply because it’s running.

It’s finished when I’ve documented the architecture, captured the operational runbook, automated what should be automated and extracted any reusable principles that might improve the next project.

The same idea applies outside engineering.

A productive conversation with AI isn’t complete when I have an answer.

It’s complete when that conversation leaves behind something useful for future me—a note, a checklist, a reusable workflow, a blog post or a better mental model.

Experience fades surprisingly quickly.

Artifacts don’t.

## AI changes the economics of capability development

Much of the discussion around AI focuses on automation.

That’s certainly valuable, but I think the more interesting opportunity is how it improves the feedback loops involved in developing capability.

When I’m designing a new service for my personal cloud, AI acts as a design reviewer.

When I’m writing, it’s an editor and thinking partner.

When I’m learning something new, it’s less of an explainer and more of a curriculum designer, helping identify what I should learn next rather than simply answering the question I’ve already asked.

When I’m reflecting on a week of work, it’s a pattern detector that helps identify recurring themes I would almost certainly have missed.

None of those replace the work.

They improve the quality of the work—and, over time, the quality of the person doing it.

## Build systems that reinforce themselves

Perhaps the strongest evidence that this approach is working is that my projects increasingly reinforce one another.

The personal cloud generates architectural experience.

That experience becomes blog posts.

Writing those posts improves my thinking.

Those ideas become templates, runbooks and reusable workflows.

Those artifacts improve the next project.

AI helps review the entire cycle, identifying patterns and suggesting where to invest next.

Nothing exists in isolation anymore.

That’s exactly how I’d expect a well-designed platform to behave.

## Design yourself like a platform

Platform teams don’t exist to build every application.

They exist to make every future application easier to build.

I increasingly think the same way about personal development.

The goal isn’t to master every technology.

The goal is to build a system that continually makes acquiring new capabilities easier.

That’s a fundamentally different objective.

One optimises for today’s skills.

The other optimises for decades of growth.

## The operating system matters more than the applications

Looking back, the biggest improvements in my career rarely came from learning one more tool.

They came from improving the underlying operating system.

Learning how to learn.

Improving judgement.

Writing more clearly.

Reflecting more deliberately.

Building stronger feedback loops.

Choosing projects that maximise Capability ROI.

Technology will continue to change.

Roles will evolve.

New disciplines will emerge.

The engineers who thrive won’t necessarily be the ones who predicted every change.

They’ll be the ones who built an operating system capable of adapting to whatever comes next.

That’s the system I’m trying to build.
